Chromebook

Apr 2nd, 2014

For past month my on the go machine has been Samsung’s ARM Chromebook and it feels, at least to me, like a perfect laptop. This thing has it all: extreme portability, a long battery life, 3G for web on the go and best of all it’s very affordable, but let’s back up a bit…

Why did I even buy a Chromebook

About a year ago when I bought a new laptop for school and work I walked into my local store and browsed for something affordable, yet powerful and I ended up with a Sony Vaio laptop, which at the time (at least I thought) would suite me perfectly, it was a tiny power house compared to my old hand-me-down laptop, but it was big — way too big — but I didn’t realize it at the time, so for years time I dragged that hell beast to school and back, but then something happened, I started to see MacBooks and smaller Lenovo smart books that could do all the stuff I was doing and sometimes more and I was stuck carrying this beast of a machine and I decided it was time for a change.

I decided that after my summer internship would be the perfect time to upgrade my laptop since I would have the money and semester was starting again, so I began a hunt for well build, cheap, small and good looking laptop (I used a lot of SSH and VPN over the summer and realized I wouldn’t need processing power, just connectivity) and I was almost set up forking over $1000+ on a MacBook Pro 11” when I stumbled into a discussion about ChrUbuntu and how people were running full fledged Linux on Chromebooks, which I had always labeled as “casual-consumers-facebook-machine” and had never even though about buying, but then I started to do research into the matter and more I read the more appealing Chromebooks were and I ended up buying Samsung’s Series 5 Chromebook.

How to setup your brand new Chromebook

First of all you must ask you, do you really even want ChrUbuntu? With it your Chromebook will act like your traditional dual-booting system, you can restart it and at boot time decide if you want to boot into Chrome OS or Ubuntu, but there is a better alternative a nifty tool called Crouton.

With Crouton you actually boot both the Chrome OS and Ubuntu at the same time. Ubuntu runs right alongside of the Chrome OS and you can switch between the two on the fly and since the Ubuntu isn’t virtualized it doesn’t eat all of your memory either.

How to install Crouton

First of all you need to enable what Google calls “Developer Mode”, in this mode you can access all the good parts of your system that are normally locked down, be aware: entering Developer Mode disabled quite a few of the Chrome OS’ standard security features, but since you probably aren’t going to be leaving your Chromebook as a public computer for anyone to hack at, you should be perfectly fine.

To enable Developer Mode on a Samsung Chromebook hold down the ESC and Refresh (F3 or “the-key-with-the-circular-arrow”) keys and tap the Power button. This will open up the Recovery Mode.

In the Recovery screen press Ctrl+D, agree to the prompt and you’ll start the transition to Developer Mode, which will erase all local data from your Chromebook and takes about 10-15 minutes.

Next the system will reboot and display a 30 second long warning that you are running in Developer Mode, you can bypass this by pressing Ctrl+D. Note: this will happen every time you boot your machine.

Next you need to download Crouton. Here is the latest release. Once you have Crouton downloaded, press Ctrl+Alt+T in Chrome OS to open crosh terminal.

In terminal type shell and press Enter to enter the actual Linux shell mode. This command only works in Developer Mode.

Now, to install Crouton all you have to do is run following command:

bash sudo startxfce4 “`

Switching between the environments is simple just press Ctrl+Alt+Shit+Back (F1) or Ctrl+Alt+Shit+Forward (F2)

If you want to exit the chroot just log out of your Ubuntu desktop. You’ll need to run the startxfce4 command again to enter the chroot again.

Now you have fully functioning Linux distro side booting with your Chrome OS and you can do whatever you like with it. Only thing you have to remember is that Samsung’s S5 Chromebook is ARM based, so everything won’t run straight out of the box, so you might have to do some configuring and building, but so far this hasn’t stopped me with.
